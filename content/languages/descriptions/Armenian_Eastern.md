## Grammar notes

### Basic clause structure and the transitive construction

In the basic transitive construction, the subject (A-argument) is always nominative, while the direct object (P-argument) can be either nominative or dative, depending on its animacy: animate objects take the dative form (1), and inanimate objects the nominative (2). Specificity and number can also influence the choice, but within this project they don’t play a great role, since most animate direct objects are human and definite, thus taking the highest position within the dative vs. nominative DOM hierarchy.

(1)	es  	tesa 		ayn	mard-u-n
I see:AOR:1SG that man-DAT-DEF
‘I saw that man.’ 

(2)	es  	tesa 			ayn	nkar-ə
I	see:AOR:1SG	that	picture[NOM]-DEF
‘I saw that picture.’

### Case system

There are five morphologically distinct case forms in Eastern Armenian: nominative, dative, instrumental, ablative and locative. Some descriptions prefer a seven-case system, but we choose here not to include accusative (see the above-mentioned nominative/dative DOM) and genitive (possessors are thus flagged with dative (3)).

(3)  	ayn	mard-u 	kyank’-ə
that	man-DAT	life[NOM]-DEF
‘The life of that man.’
